Demonstrating the Central Limit Theorem Through Stroop Task Analysis

Empirical validation of CLT using reaction time distributions

Central Limit Theorem Sampling Distributions Statistical Inference Stroop Task

Introduction

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is a structural part of inferential statistics, stating the distribution of sample means will approach a normal distribution as the sample size increases, regardless of the shape of the population distribution. This exploration uses reaction times from the Stroop task dataset (Lakens, 2021) to demonstrate the CLT in action.

The Stroop dataset contains 131 participants' reaction times (milliseconds) for congruent and incongruent stimuli. The focus of this study is on incongruent reaction times, as these tend to have greater variability suitable for this exercise. The population parameters (M = 23.005 ms, SD = 4.765 ms) serve as baselines for our samples. This measurement of the population mean is exceptionally fast, which is to be expected of individuals enrolled in military activities. The population standard deviation means that the majority of times will fall between 18.24 ms and 27.77 ms. Overall, our population reacts very quickly.

Methods

We used three main formulas to achieve numerical understanding of our data. First, we completed a small-scale sample analysis (n = 20, 100), where five random samples of 20 and five random samples of 100 were drawn from the population. For each sample, the sample mean and sample standard deviation were calculated using:

X̄ = (1/n) Σ Xᵢ

Sample Mean

s = √[(1/(n-1)) Σ (Xᵢ - X̄)²]

Sample Standard Deviation

Next, we moved to a larger sample simulation with 5,000 samples per run. Using R, a statistical computing and data analytics software, we ran 5,000 samples of both size 20 and 50. For each simulation, the mean of sample means and the standard error were computed using:

X̄̄ = (1/k) Σ X̄ᵢ

Mean of Sample Means

SE = σ / √n

Standard Error

Finally, visualizations were made of both n = 20 and n = 50. These histograms were plotted to observe the shape of the sampling distribution, labeled with Sample Mean (ms) and Frequency.

Results

Observations from the small samples were found to vary around the population mean (M = 23.005) reflecting random variability. The standard deviation ranged from 4.006 to 4.416, fluctuating more than expected. Smaller samples exhibit more variability in total, and movement was expected for their size.

Discussion

These results demonstrate the CLT perfectly. The normality of the sample means showcases that even when the individual reaction times are somewhat variable and predictable (not perfectly symmetrical), the sampling distribution of the mean is nearly normal. Increasing the size from 20 to 50 reduced the spread but had little to no effect on the results other than a slight distribution reduction.

Key Findings

  • Mean of Sample Means ≈ Population Mean: X̄̄ = 22.979 for n = 20, and X̄̄ = 22.987 for n = 50, both very close to μ = 23.005
  • Standard Error Decreases with Sample Size: SE = 1.053 (n = 20) versus SE = 0.669 (n = 50)
  • Matches Theoretical Formula: Observed SE aligns perfectly with theoretical SE = σ / √n

The implications of these findings support CLT, as CLT allows researchers to use sample means to make accurate inferences about the population mean. As the sample size increases, or more samples are collected, the estimates of μ become more reliable and less variable. This allows us to rely on sample means and the CLT for statistical inference.

Conclusion

The Stroop reaction time dataset clearly demonstrates the Central Limit Theorem. The sample means converge to a normal distribution as the sample size and number of samples increase. The mean of sample means approaches the population mean μ. The standard error of the mean decreases with larger sample sizes, as seen with n = 20 and n = 50 in our 5,000 samples test run.

This exploration illustrates how even moderately skewed data like reaction times produce normal sampling distributions, which enables valid statistical inference. The CLT provides the foundation for confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and numerous other inferential statistical techniques used across scientific disciplines.
