Sports Toxicity Index: Data-Driven Athletic Risk Assessment

Quantifying vulnerability across sport types through academic performance, retention rates, and demographic analysis

Data Analytics Statistical Analysis Bayesian ANOVA Risk Assessment

Executive Summary

This analysis examines 944 student-athletes at Heidelberg University to identify which sports create the highest academic and retention risks. The findings reveal that contact sport athletes stay with programs more than twice as long as non-contact athletes (7.09 vs. 3.76 semesters), and team sports demonstrate nearly three times the stability of individual sports (8.87 vs. 2.98 semesters). The highest-risk populations include football/track multi-sport athletes (GPAs 2.6-3.2, 25% quit rate), Pell-eligible students concentrated in single sports (43.1%) with limited support networks, and individual sport athletes facing the greatest program instability.

Dataset and Methodology

Sample Characteristics

The dataset includes 944 returning student-athletes from the 2025 school year, predominantly from Ohio (80.5%). Key variables analyzed included Term GPA (M = 3.13, SD = 0.85), Cumulative GPA (M = 3.17, SD = 0.74), sport status, coach tenure, and demographic factors. The population was 54.2% male, 82.5% Caucasian, with 43.6% Pell-eligible and 35% first-generation students. Sport participation breakdown: 61.4% active in at least one sport, 38.6% no sport, 7.1% quit, and 0.2% injured.

Toxicity Score Calculation

The toxicity framework quantified vulnerability across three dimensions: academic risk (0-3 scale based on inverse coach tenure relationship), quit risk (0-3 scale based on sport-specific quit rates), and physical risk (0-3 scale based on contact sport classification). Each sport category received a total toxicity score combining these factors with mean coach tenure data.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Contact vs. Non-Contact Sports

An independent samples t-test revealed contact sports (M = 7.09, SD = 4.086, n = 331) demonstrated significantly higher coach tenure than non-contact sports (M = 3.76, SD = 6.059, n = 779). The mean difference of 3.335 semesters (95% CI: 2.621-4.049) showed a medium-large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.601, p < 0.001). Contact sports appear to provide stronger team atmospheres or support structures promoting persistence.

Finding 2: Sport Category Stability

Bayesian ANOVA testing on sport categories showed team sports (M = 8.874, 95% CI: 8.509-9.238) had nearly three times the coaching stability of individual sports (M = 2.982, 95% CI: 2.356-3.609). Performance sports fell in between (M = 4.0, 95% CI: 3.014-4.986). Individual sport athletes face the highest program instability and would benefit from enhanced mentoring and peer networks to compensate for lack of built-in team support.

Finding 3: Pell Eligibility and Sport Participation

Pell-eligible students (43.6% of population, n = 412) were overwhelmingly concentrated in single-sport participation (n = 359, 43.1%) compared to multi-sport participation. Financially disadvantaged students lack the diversification and support systems that multi-sport athletes enjoy, creating vulnerability when concentrated in high-risk categories like individual sports.

Finding 4: Multi-Sport Athlete Analysis

Analysis of 50 three-sport athletes revealed 80% participated in seasonal variations of the same sports (track/cross-country combinations or cheerleading) rather than true diversification. Notably, 70-80% were first-generation students with 50-60% Pell-eligibility, exceeding the overall sample rate. Women's cross country/track athletes consistently showed GPAs above 3.7, while men's football/track combinations struggled academically (GPAs 2.6-3.2) with higher quit rates, indicating unsustainable academic pressure from year-round contact sport demands.

Recommendations

Evidence-Based Interventions

  • Individual Sport Athletes: Implement enhanced mentoring programs and peer connection networks to compensate for lack of built-in team support
  • Male Contact Sport Athletes: Provide additional academic support and load management, especially for football/track combinations
  • Pell-Eligible Students: Create peer networks and monitoring systems, as they face compounded financial and academic pressures without alternative athletic communities
  • Co-ed Programs: Increase program stability support to address 45% lower tenure compared to single-gender sports
  • Three-Sport Athletes: Monitor for burnout, particularly Pell-eligible students in year-round participation

Conclusion

This analysis established a three-dimensional risk framework (academic, quit, physical) quantifying vulnerability across sport types with robust statistical power (Cohen's d = 0.601). The data preparation process revealed critical quality issues requiring systematic cleaning before analysis, while maintaining ethical protocols for small-sample sport programs to protect athlete privacy. The findings enable evidence-based intervention design targeting the highest-risk 30-40% of the student-athlete population, particularly individual sport athletes, financially disadvantaged students, and multi-sport contact athletes. Future analysis should include longitudinal tracking of quit rates and academic standing to identify specific intervention points, as well as gender-specific analysis within sport categories to understand how gender interacts with sport-specific demands.
